This transformation treats image conversion as a catalyst-mediated process controlled by a separate evolving field of local activity. Source material does not transform simply because it exists next to target material; instead, conversion happens where active catalytic sites are present, and those sites can turn over repeatedly, become poisoned, fatigue, recover, or shift location. This draws inspiration from heterogeneous catalysis, enzymatic turnover, active-site poisoning, and catalyst restructuring. What makes it useful is that the speed and pattern of transition are governed by process competence rather than only concentration or proximity, producing spatially uneven behavior with bursts, stalls, and selective activation. It tests important aspects of vision because it generates patchy, event-driven transformation that challenges the visual system to detect causal structure in temporally irregular change. Regions may convert rapidly, then pause, then reactivate elsewhere, which probes perception of process agency, local versus global causality, and the legibility of latent state variables that are not directly visible but are inferred from changing appearance. The images can play an active role by defining where catalysts initially exist, where they are stable, where they poison easily, where regeneration is likely, and where feedstock is abundant. Source image features may supply reactant density, while target image features may stabilize catalytic states or act as sinks for product formation. Roughness, edge density, topology, or hidden masks can increase local active surface area or modify poisoning susceptibility. In a general implementation, define hidden fields for reactant amount, product amount, catalyst activity, and deactivation or poison load. Conversion rates depend on the local product of reactant availability and catalyst activity. Catalyst fields evolve according to turnover, poisoning, recovery, migration, or restructuring rules, all modulated by image-derived maps. Introduce nonlinearity so that some regions self-accelerate while others choke off. For PBR and multichannel use, catalyst history can drive different channels differently, allowing appearance to depend not just on current composition but on local process exposure. This is especially powerful for transitions that should feel historical, episodic, and surface-chemistry driven rather than smoothly blended.
