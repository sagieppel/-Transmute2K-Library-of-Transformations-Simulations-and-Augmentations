Bacterial biofilms grow on surfaces through a chemically regulated process where individual cells attach, proliferate, and produce a signaling molecule. When the local concentration of that molecule crosses a threshold through quorum sensing the colony switches collective behavior, either consolidating into a structured matrix or triggering dispersal where the biofilm dissolves and cells migrate to new sites. The source image is the substrate surface topology and chemistry where its pixel values define surface affinity for attachment so that the roughness channel maps to attachment probability and albedo maps to nutrient availability. Cells from a seed population attach preferentially to high-affinity sites in the source image and their local density constitutes a growing coverage field. Once coverage in a neighborhood crosses the quorum threshold that patch locks into a mature biofilm state which begins resembling the target image pixel values as the biofilm matrix encapsulates and replaces the substrate appearance. Meanwhile the dispersal signal which diffuses outward from mature patches triggers surrounding immature patches to dissolve and temporarily revert toward source values before re-colonizing. This creates a wave-like alternation between source and target across the image with mature biofilm islands that are target-like surrounded by dispersal halos that are source-like and which themselves become future colonization fronts. The process is self-regulating so that the image content controls quorum thresholds spatially and complex textures generate complex biologically realistic colonization patterns. For PBR maps the colonization coverage field and the quorum-concentration field are both spatially explicit and modulate all PBR channels simultaneously so that roughness increases as biofilm matures, albedo shifts, and the normal map develops the characteristic dome-like topology of real biofilm colonies, all from a single physically motivated field.
