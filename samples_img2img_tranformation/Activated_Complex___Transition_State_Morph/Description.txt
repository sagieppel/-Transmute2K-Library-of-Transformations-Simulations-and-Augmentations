This transformation treats the source image and target image as two metastable material states connected by a reaction pathway rather than as endpoints of a simple blend. The transition proceeds through unstable intermediate configurations that belong fully to neither image. Visually, this produces temporary motifs, reorganizations, and short-lived structures that resemble activation, bond rearrangement, or local structural reconfiguration before relaxation into the final state. It tests important aspects of vision because it probes whether structure is perceived through identity-preserving continuity or through topological and textural reorganization. It is useful for studying how observers interpret ambiguous intermediate states, how much transient disorder can be introduced before the final object or material remains recognizable, and how different channels such as color, roughness, and normal structure contribute differently to continuity of perception. In general implementation terms, the two images define local energy minima, and their differences define where activation barriers are high or low. Image features such as edges, corners, texture complexity, segmentation masks, or depth can modulate barrier height, pathway multiplicity, or local transition speed. The process can be modeled as relaxation on an image-derived energy landscape with an explicit unstable intermediate regime, where some regions overshoot, fluctuate, or temporarily enter a third state before settling. For multi-channel and PBR data, each channel can follow a different but coupled pathway, allowing albedo, roughness, normals, metallic, or subsurface-related channels to convert with distinct timing and transient signatures.
