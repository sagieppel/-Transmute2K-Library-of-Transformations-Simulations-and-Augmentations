Inspired by desert geomorphology, wind erosion science, yardang formation, and loess deposition. In hyper-arid deserts, wind performs two simultaneous geological acts: deflation (stripping away loose surface material, exposing harder bedrock below) and deposition (building up new landforms as wind-carried particles settle). Over millennia, entire landscapes are erased and rewritten by wind alone. For the transition: Image A is the 'bedrock' surface being exposed as the wind strips away the overlying material, while Image B is the 'loess'—fine sediment carried by the wind and deposited on the other side of the front. A wind front sweeps across the image, simultaneously erasing (deflating) Image A's surface and building up (mantling) Image B. The critical aesthetic: the transition front is geologically irregular, not geometrically smooth. Soft regions (low-contrast areas, smooth gradients, low-frequency content) erode first—like soft sandstone being undercut. Hard features (sharp edges, high-frequency detail, high-contrast boundaries) resist erosion and persist as streamlined ridges (yardangs). On the deposition side, particles accumulate unevenly—settling in lee-side sheltered areas first, building up dune-like forms that carry Image B's structure. The overall result looks like two geological epochs meeting: one being unmade, the other being born, with a rugged frontier between them that feels ancient and naturalistic. This tests vision's sensitivity to texture erosion patterns, edge preservation under degradation, and spatial frequency-dependent masking. Implementation involves computing local hardness maps from image features (edge density, contrast, frequency content), using them to modulate erosion rate of Image A and deposition rate of Image B along a sweeping front. Each channel represents a different geological stratum with its own hardness—harder channels erode slower, softer channels faster—so the normal map might erode before the albedo, or roughness might deposit ahead of color, mimicking how different minerals weather at different rates.
