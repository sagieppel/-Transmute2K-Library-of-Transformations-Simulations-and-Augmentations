This transformation treats the target image as a buried earlier or alternate composition hidden beneath the source image, like an artist's abandoned draft re-emerging through the visible painting. The transition should feel less like a simple blend and more like the source image gradually betraying structural contradictions: displaced contours, ghosted forms, hidden masses, earlier brush intentions, and compositional corrections. In early stages, subtle edge echoes and buried silhouettes can appear underneath the source. In middle stages, the image appears to disagree with itself, as if two intentions coexist. In late stages, the hidden composition asserts itself and becomes the new visible image. This tests important aspects of vision because human perception is highly sensitive to contour continuity, figure-ground stability, occlusion logic, and the difference between surface appearance and underlying structure. It also probes how viewers resolve ambiguous or competing forms over time. In general implementation terms, this can be built as a layered reveal process where image B is not introduced uniformly but through latent structural cues derived from correspondence between the two images. Different channels can transition at different semantic depths: depth and normals may reveal hidden relief or buried stroke direction before albedo changes, while roughness or reflectance can suggest varnish differences, paint age, or suppressed brushwork. The method works especially well when the system supports intermediate states such as underdrawing, shifted alignment fields, edge-memory maps, or region-specific reveal schedules.
