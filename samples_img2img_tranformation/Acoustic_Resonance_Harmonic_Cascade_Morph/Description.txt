The source image creates an acoustic impedance map. When driven at a fundamental frequency, regions of varying impedance create scattering, interference, and standing wave patterns. These acoustic patterns drive cavitation, sonochemistry, or mechanical resonance effects that transform the material. The morph progresses through harmonic generations where new frequencies are generated at impedance boundaries. The image acts as a waveguide determining where standing waves form. Cavitation damage and acoustic streaming create material changes preferentially at pressure antinodes. This is important for vision because it tests how wave interference patterns determine material transformation. Multiple channels can represent different acoustic modes such as longitudinal and shear waves. Depth develops as cavitation damage accumulates and standing wave patterns shift. This is distinct from Marangoni or flow-based morphs because wave interference and resonance are the primary drivers rather than surface tension gradients.
