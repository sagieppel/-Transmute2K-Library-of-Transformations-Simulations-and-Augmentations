Treat the image as a chemically reactive rock matrix undergoing fluid-driven mineralisation — a process central to hydrothermal ore deposit formation and diagenetic replacement in geology. A mineralising fluid infiltrates the image along high-permeability pathways defined by the image gradient field. Where the fluid contacts the host matrix, a replacement reaction begins: local pixel values are consumed and transformed into a new mineral phase with different optical and physical properties, releasing byproducts that either accelerate further reaction (autocatalytic) or temporarily passivate the surface (inhibitory). The replacement front advances inward from fluid pathways, but its geometry is controlled by the local reactivity — which is mapped from image channel values — and by the progressive armoring of reacted zones that slows further infiltration. This produces pseudomorphic replacement textures: the large-scale spatial structure of the original image is preserved as a ghost or relic pattern, while the fine-scale content is completely overwritten by the advancing mineral phase. Channel mixing occurs at the replacement front where fluid-borne components and matrix-derived components co-precipitate, creating compositional gradients and zoned mineral textures that encode the history of the infiltration event.
