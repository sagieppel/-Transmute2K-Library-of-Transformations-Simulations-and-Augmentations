Model the image as a mixture of charged molecular species moving through a gel or fluid medium under an applied electric field — the physical basis of electrophoresis and isoelectric focusing. Each image channel represents a different molecular species carrying a characteristic effective charge determined by its local value relative to a tunable reference pH. Species migrate toward the electrode of opposite polarity at speeds proportional to their charge-to-size ratio, but crucially, as they migrate they alter the local pH or ionic environment, which in turn changes their own charge and therefore their mobility. At the isoelectric point — where net charge is zero — a species stops migrating and focuses into a sharp band. Different channels focus at different spatial positions along the field axis, producing clean separated bands. Diffusion works against this focusing, creating a dynamic equilibrium between electrophoretic concentration and diffusive spreading. The applied field direction and strength can vary spatially, guided by image gradients, producing curved or irregular banding patterns rather than simple horizontal stripes, and allowing complex multi-channel separations that reorganize image content into spatially resolved spectral layers.
