Interpret the image as a thin elastic plate, sheet, or laminated surface that can bend and relax under internal or external loads. Instead of simply moving pixel values through diffusion or advection, the transformation is driven by mechanical energy: stiff regions resist deformation, soft regions sag or bend, and pinned or loaded regions generate long-range stress patterns. Different channels can represent displacement, stiffness, thickness, load, residual stress, or anchors. This is interesting because it explores vision-relevant aspects such as global shape coherence, nonlocal deformation, rigidity contrast, curvature sensitivity, and the relationship between local features and large-scale warping. It can produce transformations where fine structures are preserved in rigid zones while neighboring regions deform smoothly around them, making it a powerful model for studying structured distortion instead of random warp. For implementation in general terms, define an energy or PDE-based plate model using bending and possibly stretching terms, let image-derived fields control stiffness and forcing, and iteratively solve for displacement or relaxation. The transformed image can then be obtained by warping channels according to the resulting deformation or by using stress and curvature fields to modify channel values directly. This yields deformations that feel structural, constrained, and physically organized.
